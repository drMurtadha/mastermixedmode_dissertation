# Student Briefing Document

## Master of Computer Science (Mixed Mode)

## Dissertation Briefing for New Students

This briefing document provides a practical overview of what is expected from a Master of Computer Science student undertaking a mixed-mode dissertation. It is aligned with the dissertation evaluation criteria reflected in the uploaded assessment form, which assesses the dissertation through the title, abstract, introduction and research problem, objectives and scope, literature review, methodology, analysis and presentation of findings, discussion and interpretation, conclusion and recommendations, writing style, strengths, weaknesses, significance, and final recommendation. The form also shows that examiners consider originality, critical ability, evidence of new findings, presentation quality, and whether part of the work is worthy of publication. ## 1. What this dissertation really is

Your dissertation is not just a long report and not just a system development document. It is a research-based academic study. Even if your work includes software development, modelling, simulation, machine learning, or framework design, the dissertation must still answer a clear research problem.

You are expected to show that you can:

- identify a meaningful problem
- justify why the problem matters
- review related studies critically
- design a proper methodology
- analyse your findings carefully
- interpret what the results mean
- write in a formal academic manner
- show some contribution to knowledge or practice

In simple terms, your dissertation must show that you can think and work like a postgraduate student, not only like a developer.

---

## 2. What examiners will look for

Based on the assessment form, your dissertation will be judged section by section and also as a complete piece of academic work. Examiners will look at whether:

- the title matches the actual study
- the abstract is clear and concise
- the problem statement is real, focused, and justified
- the objectives are clear and measurable
- the literature review is relevant and critical
- the methodology is sound and properly explained
- the findings are presented clearly
- the discussion shows interpretation, not just repetition
- the conclusion shows contribution and maturity
- the writing is coherent and academically acceptable

They may also consider:

- originality
- evidence of new findings
- critical analysis
- quality of presentation
- publication potential  [oai_citation:1‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

---

## 3. How to begin correctly

### Start with a problem, not with a tool

Do not begin with:

- “I want to use AI”
- “I want to use blockchain”
- “I want to build an app”
- “I want to use IoT”

These are tools or technologies, not research problems.

Start instead with questions such as:

- What real issue am I trying to solve?
- Who is affected by this issue?
- Why are current approaches still insufficient?
- What exactly is missing in the current literature or practice?

A good dissertation topic must be:

- problem-driven
- academically relevant
- feasible within your time and skills
- measurable or evaluable
- narrow enough to complete properly

---

## 4. What makes a good topic

A strong topic usually has these characteristics:

- a specific problem
- a clear computing component
- a realistic scope
- available data, tools, or implementation path
- a basis for comparison, testing, or evaluation
- potential for a meaningful contribution

A weak topic usually has these problems:

- too broad
- too vague
- based only on a trendy term
- no clear research gap
- no measurable outcome
- too large for one student in one dissertation

Before your topic is approved, you should be able to answer:

- What is the exact problem?
- Why does it matter?
- What has already been done?
- What is still missing?
- What will this study contribute?
- How will success be measured?

---

## 5. How to frame your study

Your early write-up must establish a strong research foundation.

### Background

Your background should move from:

1. broad context
2. current practice
3. limitations of current practice
4. evidence from literature or real cases
5. research gap
6. reason the study matters

### Problem statement

Your problem statement must clearly explain:

- what the problem is
- why it is important
- what current methods fail to do
- why your study is needed

Avoid vague claims such as:

- “technology is growing rapidly”
- “there are many cyber attacks nowadays”
- “current systems are inefficient”

These statements are too generic unless supported and narrowed properly.

### Research questions

Your research questions must be:

- specific
- answerable
- aligned to the study
- linked to your methodology and results

### Objectives

Your objectives must be:

- clear
- measurable
- realistic
- directly linked to the method
- directly answered by the findings

Good objectives are not task lists. “To study the literature” is not a strong objective. “To evaluate the performance of the proposed model using selected benchmarks and defined metrics” is much stronger.

### Scope

You must define:

- what is included
- what is excluded
- dataset or target users
- platform or context
- technical boundaries
- evaluation boundaries

A narrow scope is a strength, not a weakness.

---

## 6. What a literature review should do

Your literature review is not a list of article summaries. It must show that you understand the field and can think critically about previous work.

A good literature review should:

- explain the field
- organise prior work into themes
- compare relevant studies
- identify strengths and weaknesses
- show what has not yet been solved
- justify your study

Your review should preferably be thematic, not paper-by-paper only. For example:

- traditional approaches
- machine learning approaches
- deep learning approaches
- evaluation methods
- research gaps

You should also prepare a synthesis table, for example with:

- author and year
- problem addressed
- dataset or context
- method used
- metrics used
- key findings
- limitations
- relevance to your study

At the end of the chapter, you must state the research gap clearly.

---

## 7. What is expected in methodology

Your methodology must explain how the study is designed and how the findings will be produced. It must not be just a list of software tools.

Depending on your study, methodology may include:

- research design
- data source or dataset
- sampling or selection process
- preprocessing
- system, model, or framework design
- implementation process
- experiment procedure
- baseline comparison
- validation approach
- evaluation metrics
- ethical considerations if relevant

A strong methodology is:

- logically connected to the objectives
- detailed enough to understand
- defensible academically
- reproducible where possible

If you state an objective, you must show how the methodology answers it.

---

## 8. What is expected in results and analysis

Results are not just screenshots, tables, or charts. Results must be organised, explained, and interpreted.

Your findings chapter should:

- follow the objectives or research questions
- present evidence clearly
- use tables and figures properly
- explain what the results show
- avoid raw output without commentary

A safe structure is:

- results for Objective 1
- results for Objective 2
- results for Objective 3

If you use evaluation metrics such as accuracy, F1-score, RMSE, MAE, latency, throughput, or usability score, you must explain:

- why the metric is suitable
- what the value means
- how it compares with a baseline or previous work

---

## 9. What is expected in discussion

The discussion chapter is where you show maturity. This is where you explain what the findings mean.

A good discussion answers:

- What do the results mean?
- Why did the method perform this way?
- How do these findings compare with previous studies?
- What do the findings imply for research or practice?
- What limitations remain?

Do not simply repeat the results chapter. Discussion must interpret, compare, and synthesise.

---

## 10. What is expected in conclusion

Your conclusion should:

- restate the study purpose briefly
- summarise the main findings
- state whether the objectives were achieved
- explain the contribution
- mention limitations
- suggest reasonable future work

Do not introduce major new information in the conclusion.

---

## 11. What “contribution” really means

Many students say “this study contributes to knowledge” without explaining how. That is too vague.

Your contribution must be stated clearly and concretely. It may be:

- a validated model
- an improved framework
- a new comparison with clear findings
- a domain-specific implementation with measured performance
- a useful methodological insight
- a justified application of a method in a new context

Simply building a system is usually not enough. The question is not only what you built, but what was learned, shown, tested, or improved.

---

## 12. Writing expectations

Your dissertation must be written in clear academic English. This does not mean complicated language. It means:

- clear structure
- precise wording
- formal tone
- consistent terminology
- logical flow
- correct citation and referencing
- no unsupported claims

Avoid:

- informal or conversational writing
- vague statements
- long confusing sentences
- poor paragraph structure
- inconsistent formatting

Do not wait until the end to start writing. Good dissertations are written and revised progressively.

---

## 13. Common mistakes to avoid

Many mixed-mode dissertations become weak because of the following:

- topic too broad
- no clear research gap
- objectives written as activities
- literature review only summarises papers
- methodology too vague
- results presented without proper analysis
- discussion repeats results
- conclusion is generic
- title does not match actual work
- poor writing quality
- no clear contribution beyond implementation

If you avoid these, your dissertation quality will improve significantly.

---

## 14. Practical progress plan

A practical 12-month direction may look like this:

### Month 1

- identify a broad area
- shortlist possible topics
- prepare an initial problem background
- collect key papers

### Month 2

- finalise topic
- draft problem statement
- draft questions, objectives, scope, and significance
- prepare proposal outline

### Month 3

- strengthen literature review
- prepare synthesis table
- define the research gap clearly

### Month 4

- finalise methodology
- identify datasets, tools, and experiment plan
- obtain supervisory approval before implementation

### Months 5 to 7

- implement the method, system, or model
- run experiments or collect data
- document all procedures carefully

### Months 8 to 9

- analyse findings
- prepare tables and figures
- compare with related studies

### Months 10 to 11

- write full dissertation draft
- improve alignment across chapters
- strengthen discussion and contribution

### Month 12

- polish abstract, title, and conclusion
- proofread and format carefully
- prepare for submission or presentation

---

## 15. What you should remember throughout

Keep these points with you from the beginning:

1. Do not choose a topic just because it sounds modern.
2. Do not write vague problem statements.
3. Do not proceed without a clear research gap.
4. Do not make objectives that cannot be evaluated.
5. Do not treat methodology as a software list.
6. Do not present results without interpretation.
7. Do not assume implementation alone is contribution.
8. Do not leave writing until the final stage.
9. Do not ignore feedback.
10. Do not lose alignment between title, problem, objectives, method, findings, and conclusion.

---

## 16. Final message to the student

Your dissertation should be built carefully from the first week. Choose a focused topic, define a real problem, review the literature critically, design a sound methodology, present and interpret results properly, and write with discipline. If you do this consistently, your dissertation will not only satisfy the required chapter structure, but also meet the deeper expectations reflected in the formal assessment, including originality, critical understanding, presentation quality, contribution, and publication potential.
