# Structured Guidance for a New Mixed-Mode Master Student

## 1. Understand the nature of a mixed-mode dissertation

A mixed-mode Master dissertation is not merely a long report. It must show that the student can define a real research problem, review literature critically, design a defensible methodology, analyse results properly, and communicate findings in academic form. The assessment form makes this very clear because it does not only judge the final outcome, but also the student’s ability to demonstrate critical understanding, scientific skills, interpretation, scholarly writing, and contribution to knowledge.  [oai_citation:0‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

Therefore, from the beginning, the student must understand this principle:

**A good mixed-mode dissertation is a small but real research study, not a software project report without research substance.**

---

## Phase 1. Topic Selection

### 2. Start with a problem, not with a technology

Most weak students begin with phrases such as:

- “I want to use AI”
- “I want to use blockchain”
- “I want to develop an app”
- “I want to use IoT”

This is the wrong entry point.

The correct entry point is:

- What is the real problem?
- Who faces the problem?
- Why is the current practice inadequate?
- What exact gap remains unresolved?

A suitable topic normally has five characteristics:

1. It addresses a real and specific problem
2. It has a clear computing component
3. It is researchable within the student’s timeframe and skill level
4. It allows data collection, experimentation, evaluation, or structured analysis
5. It can lead to a clear dissertation structure and defensible findings

### 3. Use a topic screening framework

Before approving any topic, require the student to answer these questions:

#### Problem
- What exactly is going wrong now?
- Who is affected?
- What evidence shows it is a real problem?

#### Researchability
- Can data be obtained?
- Can the method be implemented and tested?
- Can success be measured objectively?

#### Novelty
- What have others already done?
- What is still missing?
- What will be different in this study?

#### Feasibility
- Can this be completed within mixed-mode constraints?
- Are the tools, data, and skills available?
- Is the scope narrow enough?

#### Academic value
- Does the work produce knowledge, not only a product?
- Can the student compare, evaluate, explain, or validate results?

### 4. Guide the student to formulate the topic properly

A good title should reflect:

- the problem domain
- the proposed approach
- sometimes the context or dataset/application setting

Examples:

- **Weak:** AI for Healthcare
- **Better:** A Machine Learning-Based Framework for Early Classification of Diabetic Retinopathy Using Retinal Image Features
- **Weak:** Blockchain Voting System
- **Better:** A Secure Blockchain-Based E-Voting Framework with Biometric Verification for Local Election Authentication

The title must match the research content. This matters because the evaluation form explicitly assesses the relevance of the title to the research proposal and field.  [oai_citation:1‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

## Phase 2. Problem Framing and Proposal Foundation

### 5. Train the student to write the problem background correctly

The background section should move in this order:

1. Broad context
2. Current practice or current system
3. Existing limitations
4. Evidence from literature or practice
5. The unresolved gap
6. Why this gap matters

A common student mistake is writing a background that is only descriptive. The student must instead build an argument that leads naturally to the research problem.

### 6. Force precision in the research problem statement

The problem statement must not say:

- “There are many cyber attacks nowadays”
- “Technology is growing rapidly”
- “Current systems are inefficient”

These are generic statements.

A proper problem statement should identify:

- the exact issue
- its consequence
- why existing methods are insufficient
- what gap remains unaddressed

A strong template is:

> Although existing methods can do X, they still suffer from Y in the context of Z. This leads to A, B, and C. Therefore, there is a need for a method/framework/model/system that addresses Y under the conditions of Z.

This is important because the assessment rubric explicitly checks whether the candidate demonstrates critical understanding of the research problem.  [oai_citation:2‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 7. Make sure research objectives are operational

Students often confuse objectives with tasks.

**Weak objectives:**
- To study the literature
- To design a system
- To test the system

**Better objectives:**
1. To identify the critical factors affecting X in the context of Y
2. To develop a model/framework/method for addressing X
3. To evaluate the performance of the proposed model against selected benchmarks using defined metrics

Each objective must be:

- clear
- measurable
- achievable
- directly linked to methodology
- directly answered in the results chapter

### 8. Teach alignment between problem, objective, question, method, and result

A student must be able to show this chain:

**Problem → Research Gap → Research Question → Objective → Method → Evaluation Metric → Result**

If any link is broken, the dissertation becomes weak.

## Phase 3. Scope Definition

### 9. Narrow the study early

Mixed-mode students often fail because their scope is too broad. Require the student to define:

- target domain
- user group or dataset
- platform or environment
- method boundaries
- variables included and excluded
- evaluation boundaries

Examples:

- only one dataset, not five
- only one institution, not all universities
- only network traffic classification, not full security management
- only indoor positioning with BLE, not all localisation technologies

A strong scope protects the study from becoming unrealistic.

## Phase 4. Literature Review

### 10. Explain what a real literature review is

The literature review is not a list of summaries. It must do four things:

1. explain the field
2. compare prior studies
3. identify patterns and weaknesses
4. justify the current study

The assessment form specifically expects the literature review to be comprehensive, to describe relevant material, and to be critically connected with the purpose of the study.  [oai_citation:3‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 11. Require thematic review, not article-by-article narration

A strong review is organised by themes such as:

- existing approaches
- evaluation metrics
- datasets
- architectural choices
- limitations and unresolved issues

Example structure:

- Traditional approaches
- Machine learning approaches
- Deep learning approaches
- Evaluation practices
- Research gaps

### 12. Make the student build a comparison table

Require a synthesis table with columns such as:

- Author/year
- Problem addressed
- Dataset/context
- Method
- Metrics
- Main findings
- Limitation
- Relevance to current study

This helps the student move from summary to synthesis.

### 13. Push for an explicit research gap statement

At the end of the literature review, the student must clearly state:

- what has been done
- what remains insufficient
- what exact gap this dissertation addresses

Without this, the dissertation will look derivative.

## Phase 5. Methodology Design

### 14. Teach methodology as a research logic, not a tool list

Students often write methodology as:

- Python was used
- TensorFlow was used
- questionnaires were distributed

That is insufficient.

Methodology must explain:

- research design
- data source
- sampling or dataset selection
- preprocessing
- model/system/framework design
- experiment procedure
- validation strategy
- metrics
- ethical considerations if relevant

The form explicitly evaluates whether the candidate demonstrates good mastery of practical and scientific skills through methodology/design/methods to solve complex research problems.  [oai_citation:4‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 15. Require reproducibility

A strong methodology should allow another researcher to repeat the work. Tell the student to specify:

- dataset size
- feature selection
- hardware/software environment
- parameter settings
- training/validation split
- baseline methods
- number of runs
- statistical treatment if relevant

### 16. Match methodology to objective

Each objective must have a corresponding method.

For example:

- **Objective 1: identify factors** → use review, expert input, feature analysis, or exploratory analysis
- **Objective 2: develop model** → explain architecture/design process
- **Objective 3: evaluate model** → explain experiments and metrics

---

## Phase 6. Data Analysis and Results Presentation

### 17. Teach the difference between raw output and analysed findings

Students often dump screenshots, graphs, or tables without interpretation. That is not analysis.

Results must be:

- organised by objective or research question
- clearly labelled
- supported by tables/figures
- interpreted with evidence

The form separately assesses analysis and presentation of data/findings and expects effective communication using suitable methods and media.  [oai_citation:5‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 18. Require objective-driven result structure

A very safe structure is:

- Results for Objective 1
- Results for Objective 2
- Results for Objective 3

This keeps the chapter aligned and easy to defend.

### 19. Make the student justify every metric

If the student uses accuracy, F1-score, RMSE, MAE, latency, throughput, usability score, or other metrics, the student must explain:

- why that metric is appropriate
- what good performance means
- how it compares with baselines or prior work

---

## Phase 7. Discussion and Interpretation

### 20. Teach that discussion is not repetition

The discussion chapter must answer:

- What do the results mean?
- Why did the method perform this way?
- How do the findings compare with prior studies?
- What is the implication for theory, practice, or application?
- What limitations remain?

The assessment form explicitly requires the student to critically discuss and synthesise the research results/findings and to interpret and undertake critical evaluation of numerical and graphical data.  [oai_citation:6‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 21. Force comparison with literature

A good discussion includes statements such as:

- The present findings are consistent with…
- Unlike prior studies, this work shows…
- The improvement may be due to…
- The model underperformed in condition X because…

Without comparison to literature, the discussion remains shallow.

---

## Phase 8. Conclusion, Recommendations, and Contribution

### 22. Teach the conclusion as a direct answer to the study

The conclusion should:

- restate the problem
- summarise what was done
- state the main findings
- state whether objectives were achieved
- highlight contribution
- acknowledge limitations
- suggest future work

The assessment form checks the contribution towards advancing knowledge in the conclusion/recommendation section.  [oai_citation:7‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 23. Make contribution explicit

The student must state contribution in concrete form, for example:

- a validated model
- a new dataset preparation pipeline
- a comparative insight not previously reported
- an improved framework
- a domain-specific implementation with measured gains

Do not allow vague claims such as “this study contributes to knowledge”.

## Phase 9. Writing the Dissertation

### 24. Give the student a chapter-by-chapter writing plan

#### Chapter 1: Introduction

Must contain:

- background
- problem statement
- research questions
- objectives
- scope
- significance
- dissertation structure

#### Chapter 2: Literature Review

Must contain:

- conceptual and technical foundations
- review of prior studies
- synthesis and comparison
- gap identification
- conceptual framework if needed

#### Chapter 3: Methodology

Must contain:

- research design
- materials/data
- methods and procedures
- implementation details
- evaluation approach

#### Chapter 4: Results

Must contain:

- experimental or study findings
- tables/figures
- organised presentation aligned to objectives

#### Chapter 5: Discussion and Conclusion

May be separated or combined depending on format, but must contain:

- interpretation
- comparison with literature
- implications
- limitations
- conclusion
- future work

This structure matches the assessment logic in the uploaded form.  [oai_citation:8‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 25. Set writing standards from the beginning

The form includes a separate assessment for writing style. Therefore the student should be instructed to maintain:

- clear paragraph structure
- formal academic tone
- precise terminology
- consistent tense usage
- correct citation and referencing style
- no unsupported claims
- no conversational wording
- no excessive grammar errors

### 26. Train the student to write analytically

Good academic writing does not merely describe. It explains, compares, interprets, and justifies.

**Weak:**

> The model achieved 92% accuracy.

**Better:**

> The model achieved 92% accuracy, outperforming the baseline by 6.4%. This suggests that the feature engineering strategy improved class separability, especially for minority classes.

## Phase 10. Publication Orientation

### 27. Build publication thinking into supervision

The uploaded assessment form includes whether a substantial amount of findings is worthy of publication. That means the student should work from the beginning with publication discipline:

- clear novelty
- proper benchmarking
- explicit contribution
- careful result presentation
- strong discussion

A good mixed-mode student should not wait until the end to think about publication.

---

## Phase 11. Common Weaknesses to warn the student about

### 28. Typical reasons dissertations become weak

Warn the student early against these problems:

- topic too broad
- title not matching actual work
- no clear research gap
- objectives written as activities
- literature review only summarises papers
- methodology too vague to replicate
- results presented without analysis
- discussion repeats results
- conclusion introduces new information
- poor English and inconsistent formatting
- no real contribution beyond implementation

The uploaded assessment form has dedicated spaces for strengths, weaknesses, and significance, which shows that examiners will look at the work holistically, not only chapter by chapter.  [oai_citation:9‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

## Phase 12. Suggested supervision workflow for the student

### 29. Practical milestone plan

#### Month 1
- identify broad area
- shortlist 2 to 3 candidate topics
- prepare initial problem background
- collect 10 to 20 key papers

#### Month 2
- finalise topic
- draft problem statement
- draft research questions, objectives, scope, significance
- prepare proposal outline

#### Month 3
- expand literature review
- build synthesis table
- define research gap clearly
- prepare conceptual framework if needed

#### Month 4
- finalise methodology design
- identify dataset/tools/experiment plan
- get supervisor approval before implementation

#### Months 5 to 7
- implement method/system/model
- conduct experiments or collect data
- record all procedures and settings carefully

#### Months 8 to 9
- analyse results
- prepare tables and figures
- compare with related studies

#### Months 10 to 11
- write full dissertation draft
- revise introduction and literature for alignment
- strengthen discussion and contribution

#### Month 12
- polish abstract, title, conclusion
- final formatting and proofreading
- prepare for viva/presentation if required

---

## Phase 13. Supervisor’s standing advice to the student

### 30. Core instructions the student should keep throughout

1. Do not choose a fashionable topic without a real problem.
2. Do not write before the research logic is clear.
3. Do not claim novelty without evidence from literature.
4. Do not make objectives that cannot be measured.
5. Do not present results without interpretation.
6. Do not treat system development alone as research contribution.
7. Do not leave writing to the final month.
8. Do not ignore alignment across title, problem, objectives, method, and conclusion.

---

## Final supervisor message to a new student

Your dissertation must be built as an academically defensible study from the first week. Choose a topic with a clear problem, define a narrow and feasible scope, review the literature critically, design a methodology that can be justified and replicated, analyse results properly, and write with clarity and discipline. If you do these well, your dissertation will satisfy not only the chapter requirements, but also the deeper assessment expectations of originality, critical ability, presentation quality, contribution, and publication potential shown in the evaluation form.
