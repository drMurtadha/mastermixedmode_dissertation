# Formal Supervision Handbook

## Master of Computer Science (Mixed Mode)  
## Dissertation Supervision Guide for Students

This handbook sets out the supervision expectations, academic standards, workflow, and quality requirements for a Master of Computer Science student in mixed mode. It is structured to align with the dissertation assessment dimensions reflected in the uploaded evaluation form, namely: title, abstract, introduction/research problem/objectives/scope, literature review, methodology, analysis and presentation of findings, discussion and interpretation, conclusion and recommendations, writing style, strengths, weaknesses, significance, and recommendation for award. The form also indicates that examiners will consider originality, evidence of new findings, critical ability, quality of presentation, and publication-worthiness. ---

## 1. Purpose of This Handbook

The purpose of this handbook is to guide the student in conducting and writing a dissertation that meets the standard expected of a mixed-mode Master’s degree in Computer Science. The dissertation is not merely a project report or a software development document. It must demonstrate that the student can:

- identify a meaningful research problem
- formulate clear research questions and objectives
- review literature critically
- design and justify an appropriate methodology
- analyse findings systematically
- interpret results academically
- write in a clear and scholarly manner
- show a defensible contribution to knowledge or practice

The student is expected to use this handbook from the beginning of candidature until final dissertation submission.

---

## 2. Nature of a Mixed-Mode Master Dissertation

A mixed-mode Master dissertation is a small-scale but genuine research study. It may involve system development, algorithm design, modelling, simulation, experimentation, framework construction, data analysis, or empirical investigation. However, the core requirement is always the same:

**the dissertation must answer a research problem, not merely show that something was built.**

A technically functioning prototype is not enough on its own. The student must explain:

- why the work matters
- what research gap it addresses
- how the method was designed
- how performance was evaluated
- what the findings mean
- what contribution was made

This expectation is consistent with the assessment form, which evaluates not only content sections but also originality, critical judgement, and significance.  [oai_citation:1‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

---

## 3. Roles and Responsibilities

### 3.1 Student Responsibilities

The student shall:

- take primary responsibility for the progress of the dissertation
- attend meetings as scheduled and come prepared
- submit work according to agreed milestones
- revise work based on supervisory feedback
- maintain academic integrity at all times
- keep proper records of readings, data, experiments, and draft versions
- seek clarification when unsure
- ensure that claims are evidence-based and properly referenced

The student must not wait passively for direction at every stage. Mixed-mode study requires initiative, discipline, and sustained engagement.

### 3.2 Supervisor Responsibilities

The supervisor shall:

- guide the student in refining the topic and research direction
- review and comment on submitted drafts
- advise on literature, methodology, structure, and academic writing
- help the student maintain feasibility and academic focus
- identify weaknesses that require correction
- monitor progress and provide structured academic guidance

Supervision is guidance, not co-authorship of the dissertation text. The student remains responsible for the quality, accuracy, originality, and completeness of the work.

---

## 4. Principles of Dissertation Supervision

The dissertation shall be supervised according to the following principles:

### 4.1 Problem-driven research

The study must begin from a clearly articulated problem. The student must not begin with a technology trend alone.

### 4.2 Academic alignment

There must be alignment between:

- title
- problem statement
- research questions
- objectives
- scope
- methodology
- findings
- conclusion

### 4.3 Feasibility

The topic must be realistic for the duration, resources, data access, and technical ability available to the student.

### 4.4 Evidence-based reasoning

All major claims must be supported either by literature, data, experiment, or defensible analysis.

### 4.5 Critical engagement

The student must not summarise literature or results mechanically. The dissertation must show comparison, judgement, synthesis, and interpretation.

### 4.6 Contribution

The study must show a clear contribution, even if modest. Contribution may be methodological, analytical, empirical, architectural, evaluative, or application-oriented.

---

## 5. Topic Selection Policy

Topic selection is the first critical stage. A weak topic usually produces a weak dissertation regardless of later effort.

### 5.1 Requirements of a suitable topic

A suitable topic should:

- address a real and specific problem
- belong clearly to the field of Computer Science or a legitimate computing application area
- be researchable within the mixed-mode timeframe
- allow objective evaluation
- have available literature, data, tools, or implementation pathways
- support a clear dissertation structure

### 5.2 Unsuitable topic patterns

The following topic patterns are usually weak:

- overly broad topics
- topics centred only on a fashionable technology
- topics with no measurable outcome
- topics requiring unrealistic data access or infrastructure
- topics that are purely descriptive
- topics with no identifiable research gap

### 5.3 Topic approval questions

Before a topic is accepted, the student must be able to answer:

- What is the exact problem?
- Who is affected by it?
- What evidence shows the problem exists?
- What has already been done?
- What remains unresolved?
- What will this study contribute?
- How will success be measured?
- Is the scope realistic?

---

## 6. Title Development

The title must accurately reflect the work and must remain consistent with the actual content of the dissertation. The assessment form explicitly evaluates the relevance of the title to the proposed study and discipline.  [oai_citation:2‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 6.1 Characteristics of a good title

A good title is:

- specific
- academically precise
- neither too broad nor too narrow
- representative of the actual problem and approach
- free from vague promotional language

### 6.2 Title review rule

The title may be refined during the study, but it must not drift away from the actual research. Major title revision late in the candidature usually signals poor topic control.

---

## 7. Proposal and Early Research Framing

The early stage of the dissertation must establish the academic foundation of the work.

### 7.1 Background of the study

The background should explain:

- the broader domain context
- current practice or current limitation
- why the issue matters
- where the deficiency lies
- why a research response is needed

### 7.2 Problem statement

The problem statement must:

- identify the exact issue
- explain why it is a problem
- show the limitation of existing approaches
- justify the need for the present study

Generic statements are not acceptable. The student must demonstrate critical understanding of the research problem, as required by the assessment form.  [oai_citation:3‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 7.3 Research questions

Research questions must be:

- precise
- answerable
- research-oriented
- aligned with the methodology and expected findings

### 7.4 Research objectives

Objectives must be written as outcomes, not activities. Each objective must be measurable and answerable through the study.

### 7.5 Scope

The student must define what is included and excluded. A clear scope protects the study from becoming unmanageable.

### 7.6 Significance

The student must state why the study matters academically, practically, technically, or socially.

---

## 8. Literature Review Standards

The assessment form indicates that the literature review must be comprehensive, relevant, and critically connected to the purpose of the study.  [oai_citation:4‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 8.1 Purpose of the literature review

The literature review must:

- define the intellectual and technical context of the study
- identify existing methods and findings
- compare relevant prior works
- show strengths and limitations in the field
- establish the research gap
- justify the current study

### 8.2 What the literature review must not be

It must not be:

- a list of article summaries
- a sequence of disconnected paragraphs
- a collection of definitions without analysis
- a placeholder chapter written only to satisfy format

### 8.3 Required scholarly behaviours

The student shall:

- read recent and foundational literature
- organise the review thematically
- compare studies systematically
- build a synthesis table where appropriate
- identify research patterns, limitations, and unresolved issues
- end the chapter with a clear research gap statement

---

## 9. Methodology Standards

The methodology chapter is one of the most important parts of the dissertation. The form explicitly expects mastery of practical and scientific skills through methodology, materials, design, and methods.  [oai_citation:5‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 9.1 Purpose of methodology

The methodology must explain how the study was designed and conducted so that the work is academically defensible and, where possible, reproducible.

### 9.2 Methodology must include, where relevant

- research design
- dataset or data source
- sampling strategy
- system architecture or model design
- variables and parameters
- preprocessing procedures
- implementation steps
- experiment setup
- comparison baselines
- evaluation metrics
- validation approach
- ethical considerations if applicable

### 9.3 Methodology rule

The methodology must be written as a research procedure, not as a shopping list of software tools.

### 9.4 Reproducibility expectation

The student should provide enough detail for another researcher to understand how the study was conducted and how findings were generated.

---

## 10. Analysis and Presentation of Findings

The assessment form includes a dedicated component for analysis and presentation of findings and expects communication through appropriate methods and media.  [oai_citation:6‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 10.1 Findings chapter expectations

The student must:

- present findings in an organised and objective manner
- align findings to research questions or objectives
- use appropriate tables, figures, graphs, and summaries
- avoid presenting raw output without explanation

### 10.2 Findings chapter must not

- become a dump of screenshots
- repeat methodology
- contain figures without interpretation
- mix unrelated results without structure

### 10.3 Data presentation quality

Every table and figure must:

- have a clear title
- be referred to in the text
- support an analytical point
- be explained properly

---

## 11. Discussion and Interpretation Standards

The form separately evaluates discussion and interpretation, including critical discussion, synthesis, and interpretation of numerical and graphical data.  [oai_citation:7‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 11.1 Purpose of the discussion

The discussion chapter explains what the findings mean. It must go beyond description.

### 11.2 The student shall discuss

- whether objectives were achieved
- why the results occurred
- how findings compare with previous studies
- what strengths and limitations are evident
- what implications arise from the findings

### 11.3 Weak discussion patterns

The following are weak:

- merely restating results
- offering unsupported explanations
- avoiding comparison with prior work
- failing to acknowledge limitations

---

## 12. Conclusion and Recommendations

The assessment form expects the conclusion section to show contribution towards advancing knowledge.  [oai_citation:8‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 12.1 The conclusion must

- restate the study purpose briefly
- summarise the main findings
- state whether objectives were achieved
- explain the main contribution
- acknowledge limitations
- recommend future work logically

### 12.2 The conclusion must not

- introduce major new results
- become a copy of the abstract
- make exaggerated claims
- avoid limitations entirely

---

## 13. Writing Style and Academic Expression

The form includes a dedicated evaluation for writing style.  [oai_citation:9‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 13.1 Writing expectations

The dissertation must be written in:

- clear academic English
- precise technical language
- coherent paragraph structure
- logical progression
- consistent terminology
- correct citation and referencing style

### 13.2 The student must avoid

- informal language
- vague claims
- unsupported statements
- excessive grammatical errors
- inconsistent formatting
- abrupt transitions
- overlong and confusing sentences

### 13.3 Writing discipline

Writing should begin early. The student must not postpone writing until all experiments are finished.

---

## 14. Originality and Contribution

The uploaded form indicates that examiners may consider originality through substantial original contribution, new facts, clear critical ability, and publishable findings.  [oai_citation:10‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 14.1 Acceptable forms of contribution

Contribution may include:

- a new framework or model
- a validated improvement over existing methods
- a novel integration of techniques
- a new application of a method to a justified context
- a comparative finding with strong evidence
- a useful analytical insight supported by data

### 14.2 Unacceptable contribution claims

The following are usually not enough on their own:

- “I built a system”
- “I used AI”
- “I created a dashboard”
- “I used a new dataset” without analytical value

The student must show why the work matters beyond implementation alone.

---

## 15. Strengths, Weaknesses, and Significance

The dissertation will be judged holistically. The form contains separate spaces for strengths, weaknesses, and significance.  [oai_citation:11‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

### 15.1 The student should aim for these strengths

- clear problem focus
- sound academic alignment
- critical literature review
- defensible methodology
- meaningful findings
- mature discussion
- coherent writing
- clear contribution

### 15.2 The student should actively avoid these weaknesses

- broad and unclear scope
- poor gap formulation
- objectives not aligned with methods
- superficial literature review
- vague methodology
- weak evaluation
- descriptive discussion
- poor language control

### 15.3 Significance of the study

The significance section should explain why the study matters in concrete terms. It should identify:

- who benefits
- what knowledge is added
- what practice may improve
- what future work it enables

---

## 16. Meeting and Submission Protocol

### 16.1 Meeting preparation

For each supervision meeting, the student must submit in advance where possible:

- agenda or questions
- latest draft or section under review
- list of changes since the previous meeting
- specific issues requiring guidance

### 16.2 Draft submission rule

Drafts sent for review must be:

- sufficiently complete for meaningful feedback
- formatted properly
- proofread before submission

Supervisory time should not be spent correcting avoidable carelessness.

### 16.3 Revision discipline

The student must revise carefully and systematically. Repeated recurrence of previously corrected issues indicates poor engagement.

---

## 17. Suggested Dissertation Development Sequence

A practical sequence is as follows:

### Stage 1

- identify topic area
- refine problem
- shortlist literature

### Stage 2

- finalise title
- draft background, problem, objectives, scope, significance

### Stage 3

- conduct structured literature review
- prepare synthesis and gap statement

### Stage 4

- design and justify methodology

### Stage 5

- implement, experiment, collect data, or conduct empirical work

### Stage 6

- analyse results
- prepare tables and figures

### Stage 7

- discuss findings
- draft conclusion and future work

### Stage 8

- revise whole dissertation for alignment, clarity, and quality

---

## 18. Common Causes of Delay or Weak Performance

The most common causes are:

- late topic stabilisation
- poor understanding of the research problem
- insufficient reading
- repeated change of direction
- weak methodology planning
- late implementation
- lack of regular writing
- failure to respond properly to feedback

The student is advised to maintain continuity and not work only near deadlines.

---

## 19. Final Quality Standard

Before submission, the dissertation should be able to answer these questions clearly:

- Is the title accurate?
- Is the abstract concise and representative?
- Is the problem real, specific, and justified?
- Are the objectives clear and measurable?
- Is the literature review critical and gap-driven?
- Is the methodology defensible and sufficiently detailed?
- Are findings presented clearly?
- Are results interpreted critically?
- Is the contribution explicit?
- Is the writing mature and coherent?

These questions correspond directly to the assessment logic embedded in the uploaded evaluation form.  [oai_citation:12‡SKM_C650i26032611060.pdf](sediment://file_000000001808720b895bb6f0bc0818f9)

---

## 20. Closing Statement to the Student

The dissertation is the clearest evidence of the student’s maturity as a postgraduate candidate. It must show discipline, depth, technical competence, critical thinking, and academic integrity. A successful mixed-mode dissertation is not produced by last-minute writing or by technical implementation alone. It is produced through sustained and structured engagement with a real research problem, careful reading, defensible method design, disciplined analysis, and clear scholarly writing. The standards set in this handbook are intended to help the student meet the level expected in the formal dissertation assessment.
